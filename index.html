The Roadmap

How We Win

This isn't theoretical. The legal tools exist. The political pressure points are real. Other cities have done it.

The Five Demands

  1. Full Disclosure Publish every active contract between NYC agencies and Google, including costs, terms, data-sharing provisions, and renewal dates.
  2. Terminate and Transition Invoke termination-for-convenience clauses in all Google contracts and begin a managed 3-year transition to self-hosted, publicly-owned infrastructure.
  3. Protect Students First Immediately begin migrating NYC public schools off Google Workspace to privacy-respecting, open-source alternatives.
  4. Create the NYC Digital Infrastructure Office Establish a new city office responsible for managing publicly-owned technology, accountable to the public and the City Council.
  5. Ban Corporate Cloud for Critical Systems Pass legislation prohibiting city agencies from running critical infrastructure on platforms owned by companies holding military or immigration enforcement contracts.

The Legal Levers

Termination-for-convenience clauses exist in city contracts. Standard provisions that allow the city to end a contract with 30 days' notice, for any reason. No breach required. No lawsuit. Just a letter.

Comptroller Brad Lander can refuse to register contracts he deems not in the public interest. No mayor's permission needed. No City Council vote.

The City Council Technology Committee, chaired by Council Member Carmen De La Rosa, can hold hearings, subpoena documents, and draft legislation.

The exit clauses already exist. The Comptroller can block new contracts tomorrow. The City Council can legislate. The only thing missing is pressure.
