# What We Found in NYC's Checkbook *February 26, 2026* Earlier this month, the NYC Comptroller's office responded to one of our FOIL requests by pointing us to Checkbook NYC — the city's public spending database. Their message: contract data is already public. Just search for it. So we did. This is what 94 Google-related contracts across 10+ city agencies looks like. --- ## The Numbers - **94 contracts** identified - **$5 million+** in confirmed spending - **16 years** of continuous renewals (2010–2026) - **10+ agencies** including DoITT, DOT, Social Services, Environmental Protection These are floor numbers, not ceilings. The real total is obscured by subcontractor relationships, master agreements, and a purchasing system designed to hide the full scope. --- ## The Middleman Problem Here's what jumps out immediately: the city doesn't contract with Google directly. It routes everything through IT resellers. This creates a layer of separation that makes oversight harder and true spending harder to track. **CDW Government LLC** holds 35+ contracts for Google products. When DoITT wants Google Cloud, Google Workspace, or reCAPTCHA, they don't call Mountain View. They call CDW. Other major vendors: - **SHI International** — Maps products for Transportation, Homeless Services - **DASTON Corporation** — Google Workspace for Social Services - **Tempus Nova** — Platform services for DoITT These vendors hold the contract terms. They control the pricing. They manage the relationship. The city — and by extension, the public — is several steps removed from the actual agreements. --- ## What We're Actually Buying The contracts fall into clear categories: **Google Cloud Platform** — Multi-year enterprise agreements providing cloud infrastructure across agencies. Used for data storage, machine learning, and application hosting. **Google Workspace** — Thousands of enterprise licenses for city workers. Gmail, Drive, Calendar, Docs. The daily productivity stack for agencies citywide. **Google Maps APIs** — Location services embedded in DOT applications, HPD permitting systems, and planning tools. **Google Earth Engine** — Environmental Protection uses this for flood modeling and satellite analysis of NYC infrastructure. **reCAPTCHA** — Security product deployed citywide. Every city website using CAPTCHA feeds interaction data to Google. Multiple renewals 2019–2026. **Google Analytics** — Tracking code on city websites including the MyCity platform. NYC resident web activity flows to Google servers. This isn't a few isolated contracts. It's infrastructure. The city has built its digital operations on Google's stack, and the contracts show 16 years of uninterrupted renewals. --- ## The Procurement Pattern The contract IDs tell a story: CT185820181428099 becomes CT185820191401378 becomes DO185820252007426. Each renewal extends the relationship without requiring new competitive bids. Most contracts carry procurement labels like "M/WBE SMALL PURCHASE" or "INTERGOVERNMENTAL PROCUREMENT." These categories exempt agencies from full RFP processes. Public competition is waived. Alternatives aren't considered. Centralization is total. DoITT — the Department of Information Technology and Telecommunications — holds the master agreements. Individual agencies don't negotiate with Google or its resellers. They request services through DoITT, which operates under existing contracts. The result: one department controls the city's relationship with one of the world's largest surveillance companies, and the public has limited visibility into what that relationship actually costs. --- ## The Transparency Gap Checkbook NYC gives us amounts, dates, and vendor names. It doesn't give us: - **Full contract terms** — What's the actual service agreement? - **Data provisions** — What can Google collect? What can't it? - **Privacy riders** — Are there protections for NYC resident data? - **Termination clauses** — Can the city actually switch vendors? - **True total spend** — Subcontracts and master agreements hide the full number This is the design. Public databases show enough to claim transparency while hiding the details that matter. We can see Google takes our money. We can't see what the city gets in return, or what the city gives up. --- ## What's Next We've published the contract IDs. The data is downloadable as CSV. Researchers, journalists, and interested New Yorkers can see what agencies are involved and how much is flowing to which vendors. But the contracts themselves — the actual agreements, not the spending records — remain inaccessible. That's the next target. FOIL requests are pending for full contract documents, including terms, data provisions, and privacy protections. This is why we campaign for public infrastructure. When the city owns its technology, contracts are public by default. When the city rents from Google through three layers of vendors, transparency is impossible by design. The contracts exist. The spending is real. The infrastructure is built. The question is whether New Yorkers get a say in how it's run — or whether $5 million (and likely much more) flows out of public view, year after year, with no competitive alternatives and no clear exit path.
